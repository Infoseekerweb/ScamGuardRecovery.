üö© Universal Red Flags

These warning signs appear in nearly ALL scams. If you spot even one, proceed with extreme caution.

üéØ

Too Good to Be True

What it looks like:

  • "Guaranteed" returns on investments
  • Easy money with minimal effort
  • "Risk-free" opportunities
  • Exclusive deals you're "lucky" to access
  • Prices drastically below market value
Golden Rule: If it sounds too good to be true, it definitely is.
‚è∞

Urgency & Pressure

What it looks like:

  • "Act now or miss out!"
  • "Limited time offer expires today"
  • "This is your last chance"
  • Pressure to decide without research
  • Consequences for not acting immediately
Remember: Legitimate opportunities allow time for research. Scammers create urgency to prevent you from thinking clearly.
üí≥

Unusual Payment Methods

What it looks like:

  • Gift cards (iTunes, Google Play, Amazon)
  • Cryptocurrency to unknown wallets
  • Wire transfers (Western Union, MoneyGram)
  • Cash or money orders
  • Payment apps to strangers (Venmo, Cash App)
Why it matters: These payment methods are untraceable. Legitimate businesses use credit cards or checks.
üìû

Unsolicited Contact

What it looks like:

  • Random emails about prizes or opportunities
  • Cold calls claiming you owe money
  • DMs from "investors" or "recruiters"
  • Text messages about package deliveries
  • Pop-ups claiming you won something
Rule of thumb: If they contacted you first about money, it's likely a scam.
üîê

Requests for Personal Info

What it looks like:

  • Asking for passwords or PINs
  • Requesting SSN before any legitimate need
  • Wanting bank account details upfront
  • Asking for ID or passport photos
  • Remote access to your computer
Never share: Passwords, SSN, or bank details unless YOU initiated contact and verified legitimacy.
üìù

Poor Communication Quality

What it looks like:

  • Spelling and grammar errors in "official" emails
  • Generic greetings ("Dear Customer")
  • Mismatched sender addresses
  • Unprofessional logos or branding
  • Strange phone numbers or domain names
Check carefully: Legitimate companies have professional communications. Look-alike domains are common (paypa1.com vs paypal.com).
ü§´

Requests for Secrecy

What it looks like:

  • "Don't tell anyone about this opportunity"
  • "Keep this between us"
  • Discouraging you from seeking advice
  • "Your bank/family will try to stop you"
  • Claiming others "wouldn't understand"
Red alert: Legitimate businesses want you to make informed decisions. Scammers isolate victims.
üïµÔ∏è

No Verifiable Information

What it looks like:

  • No physical business address
  • Phone numbers that don't work
  • Recently created websites (check domain age)
  • No legitimate online reviews
  • Can't find them on official registries
Verify always: Real businesses have verifiable information. Use Google, BBB, state business registries.

üìä Scam-Specific Red Flags

‚Çø Cryptocurrency & Investment Scams

+

üö® Warning Signs:

  • Promises of guaranteed high returns (10%+ monthly)
  • Pressure to "invest now" before you miss out
  • Requests to send crypto to "verify" your wallet
  • Fake celebrity endorsements or testimonials
  • MLM/pyramid structure ("recruit others to earn")
  • Can deposit easily but can't withdraw
  • Platform has professional look but no regulation

‚úÖ How to Verify:

  • Check if the platform is registered with financial regulators
  • Search platform name + "scam" on Google and ScamGuard Recovery
  • Ask for company registration number and verify it
  • Check domain age (newer = riskier)
  • Never invest more than you can afford to lose
Real Example: "Bitcoin Doubler" websites promising to 2x your crypto in 24 hours. They pay early investors with new investor money (Ponzi scheme), then disappear.

üíî Romance Scams

+

üö® Warning Signs:

  • Professes love very quickly (within days/weeks)
  • Claims to be overseas (military, oil rig, doctor abroad)
  • Photos look model-perfect (often stolen images)
  • Avoids video calls with excuses
  • Eventually has "emergency" requiring money
  • Asks for gift cards, wire transfers, or crypto
  • Patterns: promises to visit but cancels, needs money for travel/medical/legal issues

‚úÖ How to Verify:

  • Reverse image search their photos (Google Images)
  • Insist on video call - real people won't consistently avoid it
  • Ask specific questions about their "location" - scammers slip up
  • Never send money to someone you haven't met in person
  • Share the situation with friends/family for perspective
Real Example: "Military soldier" on dating app develops relationship, then needs money to leave country, pay for satellite phone, or handle emergency. Photos are stolen from real soldier's social media.

üé£ Phishing Scams (Email/Text)

+

üö® Warning Signs:

  • Urgent subject lines about account problems
  • Sender email doesn't match official domain
  • Generic greeting instead of your name
  • Links don't match the claimed destination (hover to check)
  • Threatening consequences (account closure, legal action)
  • Spelling/grammar errors in "official" communication
  • Requests to "verify" or "confirm" personal information

‚úÖ How to Verify:

  • Check sender email carefully (amaz0n.com vs amazon.com)
  • Hover over links to see real destination before clicking
  • Go directly to the official website instead of clicking email links
  • Call the company using number from their official website
  • Check for HTTPS and correct domain name on login pages
Real Example: Email from "PayPal" saying account suspended, with link to "verify." Link goes to paypa1-secure.com (fake site) that steals your login and credit card.

üíº Job Offer Scams

+

üö® Warning Signs:

  • Job offer without application/interview
  • Salary seems too high for the work required
  • Requests payment for "training materials" or "equipment"
  • Asks for bank details before official hire
  • Communication only through email/text, no phone calls
  • Company has no verifiable online presence
  • Job involves receiving/reshipping packages
  • Asked to deposit checks and wire most money elsewhere

‚úÖ How to Verify:

  • Research company thoroughly - check LinkedIn, Glassdoor
  • Verify company address and phone number
  • Call the company directly using official number
  • Real employers never ask for payment upfront
  • Be skeptical of work-from-home jobs requiring no experience
Real Example: "Amazon work from home" job offer via email. Requires $200 for "starter kit." No such position exists, money goes to scammers.

üíª Tech Support Scams

+

üö® Warning Signs:

  • Pop-up claiming your computer has viruses
  • Unsolicited call from "Microsoft" or "Apple"
  • Urgent warnings with loud alerts/sounds
  • Requests remote access to your computer
  • Claims to have detected serious security threats
  • High-pressure sales for "protection software"
  • Payment required immediately to "fix" issues

‚úÖ How to Verify:

  • Microsoft/Apple NEVER call customers unsolicited
  • Close suspicious pop-ups with Task Manager (Ctrl+Alt+Del)
  • Never give remote access to unsolicited callers
  • Run your own antivirus scan from trusted software
  • If you gave access, disconnect internet and get professional help
Real Example: Pop-up locks browser with message "CALL MICROSOFT: 1-800-XXX-XXXX." Calling connects to scammers who sell fake antivirus or install malware.

üè† Rental & Real Estate Scams

+

üö® Warning Signs:

  • Price significantly below market rate
  • "Landlord" is overseas and can't show property
  • Requests deposit before viewing
  • Payment via wire transfer or gift cards
  • Listing copied from legitimate site
  • Pushes for quick decision
  • Won't meet in person or do video tour

‚úÖ How to Verify:

  • Insist on viewing property in person
  • Verify ownership through public records
  • Use reverse image search on listing photos
  • Never send money before seeing property and meeting landlord
  • Research comparable rental prices in the area
Real Example: Beautiful apartment listed for $800/month in expensive area. "Landlord" is overseas, needs deposit to "hold" it. Property is real but listing is fake - scammer stole photos.

üß† Trust Your Gut

Your Instincts Are Powerful

Studies show that when people feel "something's not right," they're correct over 90% of the time. Your brain picks up on subtle inconsistencies before you consciously recognize them.

If you're feeling:

  • üò∞ Anxious or uncomfortable
  • ü§î Confused by complicated explanations
  • üòï Skeptical despite "proof"
  • üò¨ Pressured to decide quickly
  • ü§ê Reluctant to tell friends/family

STOP. Take a break. Ask for help.

Golden Rules:

  1. When in doubt, don't. Better to miss an opportunity than lose money.
  2. Sleep on it. Legitimate offers will still be there tomorrow.
  3. Get a second opinion. Talk to someone you trust.
  4. No shame in being cautious. Smart people verify before trusting.
