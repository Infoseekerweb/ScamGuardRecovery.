What should I do immediately after being scammed?

+

Act fast! Time is critical. Follow these steps in order:

Step 1: Stop All Contact (Immediate)

  • Block the scammer on all platforms
  • Do NOT send any more money (even if they ask for "fees" to release funds)
  • Do NOT try to negotiate or recover funds yourself

Step 2: Contact Financial Institutions (First 24 Hours)

  • Bank/Credit Card: Call fraud department immediately - some transactions can be reversed
  • Wire Transfer: Contact the sending company (Western Union, MoneyGram, etc.)
  • Cryptocurrency: Document wallet addresses but recovery is unlikely
  • Payment Apps: Report to Venmo, PayPal, Cash App, Zelle - some have buyer protection

Step 3: Report to Authorities (First 72 Hours)

  • Local police department - get a case number
  • FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center: ic3.gov
  • Federal Trade Commission: ftc.gov/complaint
  • For crypto: CFTC.gov

Step 4: Document Everything

  • Screenshot all conversations
  • Save emails and text messages
  • Record transaction details
  • Note websites, usernames, phone numbers
  • Create a timeline of events

Step 5: Secure Your Accounts

  • Change passwords on all financial accounts
  • Enable two-factor authentication
  • Monitor credit reports for suspicious activity
  • Consider a credit freeze if identity was compromised

Step 6: Seek Support

  • Report to ScamGuard Recovery to warn others
  • Email us for guidance: onlyforwork597@gmail.com
  • Consider speaking with a licensed attorney
  • Reach out to friends/family for emotional support

‚è∞ Time matters! The sooner you act, the better your chances. Email us at onlyforwork597@gmail.com if you need help with any of these steps.

Common recovery scam tactics:

  • ‚ùå Promises guaranteed recovery (no one can guarantee this)
  • ‚ùå Requires upfront payment or "processing fees"
  • ‚ùå Claims to have "inside connections" or "special tools"
  • ‚ùå Pressures you to act quickly
  • ‚ùå Found you through your public scam report

Legitimate recovery typically involves:

  • ‚úÖ Law enforcement agencies (free)
  • ‚úÖ Your bank's fraud department (free)
  • ‚úÖ Licensed attorneys (transparent fees, no guarantees)
  • ‚úÖ Official restitution programs (through courts)

If contacted by a "recovery service":

  1. Do NOT engage or send money
  2. Do NOT share personal information
  3. Report them to us: onlyforwork597@gmail.com
  4. Block all communication

What are my realistic chances of getting my money back?

+

Honest truth: Recovery rates vary significantly based on several factors. Here's what research shows:

Recovery by Payment Method:

  • Credit Card: 60-90% (best protection - dispute charges immediately)
  • Bank Transfer (recent): 30-50% (if reported within 24 hours)
  • Wire Transfer: 10-20% (difficult but file reports anyway)
  • Cryptocurrency: 5-10% (nearly impossible - blockchain is permanent)
  • Gift Cards: Less than 5% (virtually unrecoverable)
  • Cash/Money Order: Less than 1% (untraceable)

Factors that improve recovery chances:

  • Reporting within hours, not days or weeks
  • Having detailed documentation
  • Scammer used traditional banking systems
  • Multiple victims reporting the same scammer
  • Law enforcement prioritizing the case

Even if recovery is unlikely:

Still report to authorities and to us. Your report can:

  • Help catch the scammers
  • Contribute to restitution funds
  • Protect future victims
  • Build a case for law enforcement
  • Provide closure through helping others

How can I tell if something is a scam?

+

Universal Red Flags (Present in almost ALL scams):

üö© Too Good to Be True

Guaranteed returns, easy money, risk-free investments, exclusive opportunities that seem unrealistic

üö© Urgency and Pressure

"Act now," "Limited time," "This opportunity won't last," pressure to decide immediately without research

üö© Requests for Unusual Payment

Gift cards, cryptocurrency, wire transfers, cash, payment apps to unknown parties

üö© Unsolicited Contact

They reached out to you first (especially about money, prizes, or opportunities)

üö© Requests for Personal Information

Asking for passwords, SSN, bank details, ID photos before any legitimate reason exists

üö© Poor Communication Quality

Spelling errors, grammar mistakes, generic greetings in "official" communications

üö© Requests for Secrecy

"Don't tell anyone," "Keep this confidential," discouraging you from seeking advice

üö© No Verifiable Information

No physical address, phone number disconnects, website created recently, no online presence

Quick Verification Steps:

  1. Search the company/person on ScamGuard Recovery
  2. Google the phone number, email, or website + "scam"
  3. Check official business registries
  4. Verify the domain name (scammers use look-alikes)
  5. Ask someone you trust for their opinion

üí° Trust Your Gut: If something feels off, it probably is. Better to miss a real opportunity than fall for a scam.

How can I protect my elderly parents/relatives from scams?

+

Seniors are disproportionately targeted. Here's how to protect them:

Education and Communication:

  • Have regular conversations about common scams
  • Share examples from ScamGuard Recovery
  • Establish a "family code word" for emergency verification
  • Make them comfortable asking for second opinions

Practical Safeguards:

  • Add yourself to bank accounts for monitoring (with their permission)
  • Set up transaction alerts on their accounts
  • Register on Do Not Call list: donotcall.gov
  • Enable spam filters on email and phone
  • Consider power of attorney for financial decisions if appropriate

Simple Rules to Teach:

  • "Never give personal information over the phone unless YOU initiated the call"
  • "The IRS/government never calls demanding immediate payment"
  • "You can't win a lottery you didn't enter"
  • "Real companies don't ask for payment in gift cards"
  • "When in doubt, hang up and call me"

Technology Help:

  • Install ad blockers and antivirus software
  • Set browser to warn about suspicious sites
  • Create email filters for common scam keywords
  • Show them how to verify sender email addresses
